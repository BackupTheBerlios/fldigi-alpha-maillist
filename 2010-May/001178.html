Correct and the Fldigi frequency display in question isn't intended to display 
the actual transmitted frequencies. Although this is an area that 
inexperienced and untrained hams always have trouble with. Again, it's a 
training issue. Hams need to know what the software and hardware they are 
putting on the air is doing. It's not idiot proof and never will be.

> Uh, no. If I'm tuned to 14.230 MHz on the radio, and I'm receiving a FAX
> signal that is AFSK between 1500 Hz and 2300 Hz, then the transmission is,
> in fact, on 14.230 Mhz - the "center" (1900 Hz, or 14.2319 MHz) is not only
> *not* where the radio is tuned, if it *was* tuned there (you'd have to
> offset the fax modem to do it), then if the operator of the transmitter
>  were to speak up and tell you "that was a fax of the important document
>  you asked for", you wouldn't be able to understand them, because you're
>  incorrectly tuned. Furthermore, if you were to send a fax back, you
>  typically want the radio on 14.230 as well; otherwise, you have to operate
>  split.
> 
> The correct frequency IS the frequency the radio is tuned to. Not whatever
> fldigi calculates as the modem center. This stuff is AFSK. Note the AF.

The radio dial frequency is the readout in Fldigi. The AFSK waterfall 
frequency, added to that radio dial frequency, for the various audio tones, is 
the frequency, or frequencies, of actual transmission. The operating ham needs 
to know this. Many times it is not a single frequency, but several or a range. 

> There are numerous reasons to receive signals at the tones they were
> designed to be sent at. If you have external gear of almost any kind, RTTY,
> packet, SSTV, FAX, etc, they're going to be expecting very specific AF
> tones. The tones you'll get when your radio is tuned appropriately, and
>  when the signal falls on the AF indications on the waterfall that are the
>  design points for the mode - for FAX, that's 1500 hz and 2300 hz. For
>  SSTV, 1200, 1500...2300 Hz. For RTTY, etc.
> 
> > Fldigi does calculate and display the
> > actual center frequency only in the log window.

Yes.

> 
> It's a misleading number at best. The correct frequency is the one the
>  radio is telling you. For optimum operations, the waterfall should be set
>  to AF, the modems should be at the design points, and the frequency
>  display is misleading and/or duplicates the radio display, consumes UI
>  space that could be better used for something useful, and should probably
>  be disabled as a direct consequence. Speaking of any situation where we
>  are not just entertaining ourselves, but are working in circumstances
>  where correct operation may have social or human consequences. There is no
>  benefit to fldigi's frequency display when it is duplicating the radio's;
>  there is no benefit when it is displaying a misleading "center frequency,"
>  either. If you were using it to preview fldigi memories, that I can see.
>  Otherwise... I have yet to be presented with a point in its favor.

For one thing, it's live. It controls the radio remotely if you have CAT 
working. It's a confirmation where the radio dial is set. I rarely touch the 
radio when doing digital. You don't need to.

> > Request: I would like the option of having the main fldigi display
> > show the actual transmission center frequency for the mode.

That is not and never was it's intended purpose. It is designed to control the 
CAT of the radio, and/or to indicate the carrier reinsertion frequency of the 
rig, for waterfall audio frequency addition or subtraction for logging.

Besides, for some digital modes the frequency stated for nets and skeds is 
definitely *not* the center frequency of the tone(s) transmitted. For some it's 
the start tone, or the others the mark tone.

You are missing the entire intended purpose of the display of frequency. It 
would appear for your purposes maybe you need to just shut it off.

It works as intended and that operation is clear to anyone who has taken the 
time to read the manual and to understand how the digital modes that they are 
transmitting work. 

Being inexperienced is no substitute for knowing what you are doing with any 
transmitter you are controlling.

Again, this is a training issue. Get the operators trained so that they know 
what they are doing and more importantly where their transmitted signal is 
going.
