Uh, no. What you have described is the convention used by amateur
radio operators, not what really happens physically. Here is an
example.

We have 50 stations operation PSK on 20m. All have their radios tuned
to 14,070 kHz. None of them are interfering with each other. By your
reckoning, they are all on the same frequency because they have tuned
the big knob on their transceiver until the number on the display
reads 14,070 kHz. OTOH, if each of them uses a frequency counter to
measure the carrier frequency they are radiating, they are each going
to get a different number, something between 14,070 and 14,073 kHz.

So the actual RF radiated is the sum of the low IF signal injected at
the mic jack, e.g. 1.500 kHz, and the dial frequency of the radio,
e.g. 14,070 kHz. You would hold that is 14,070 kHz and I would hold
that it is 14,071.5 kHz.

> There are numerous reasons to receive signals at the tones they were
> designed to be sent at. If you have external gear of almost any kind, RTTY,
> packet, SSTV, FAX, etc, they're going to be expecting very specific AF
> tones. The tones you'll get when your radio is tuned appropriately, and when
> the signal falls on the AF indications on the waterfall that are the design
> points for the mode - for FAX, that's 1500 hz and 2300 hz. For SSTV, 1200,
> 1500...2300 Hz. For RTTY, etc.

That is the problem of the receiver, not what goes out over the air.
To me all the following are the same:

Dial          low IF (tone)

14,070      3.0
14,071      2.0
14,072      1.0

As they all produce a carrier at 14,073 kHz. The operator then choses
the proper receiver injection frequency (dial setting) to produce an
IF output that the demodulator expects. Again, that is the
*operator's* problem. What I want to know is the actual frequency of
the emitted RF as that is what the FCC or anyone else is going to care
about.

Even fldigi pays homage to this concept. That is what the QSY button
does. It retunes the radio to produce the IF signal at the same
frequency no matter what.

>> Fldigi does calculate and display the
>> actual center frequency only in the log window.
>
> It's a misleading number at best. The correct frequency is the one the radio
> is telling you.

Au contraire! What fldigi reports is that actual carrier frequency,
the frequency one would measure with a frequency counter connected to
the transmitter output!

> For optimum operations, the waterfall should be set to AF,

You actually believe what you wrote there, right? You aren't just
yanking my chain? If not, please tell me the proper modem design point
for PSK31, Olivia, DominoEX, JT65, etc.

One of the things I used to do was set up VSATs. The frequency we were
assigned was the center of the transmitted signal, not the frequency
of the LO injection. As far as I can discern, the only place where
"dial frequency" is "transmit frequency" is amateur radio. Everyone
else uses the center of the transmission.

Regardless, I would be quite happy with the display being the
start-frequency or the mark-frequency in an *FSK (2-FSK, m-FSK, ISK).
And I know that the display in fldigi is the frequency sent to the
rig. But if you think about it, the value sent to the PLL or DDS in
the radio is NOT the display on the front of the rig. The value in the
VFO is actually the sum (difference) between the VFO injection
frequency and the IF frequency. In the case of fldigi we are dealing
with an IF of typically between 0kHz and 3kHz. (OK, call it an IF
frequency of 1.5kHz.) That is what the frequency readout should
display (in my world).

This is why I suggested it as an option. I want the display in fldigi
to best reflect the physical transmission, not an arbitrary value
agreed upon because that was what made the calculation easier 50 years
ago.

And I agree with Rick, K9AO, 100% on one thing: this is an education
issue. Operators need to know precisely where their signal is going.
That is why I am asking for the option to display a center frequency
as an option in addition to the dial frequency of the radio. The dial
frequency of the radio is already displayed on the radio. No need to
repeat it on fldigi (except for troubleshooting purposes).

Here is another way to look at it. When I see a 500Hz blank spot in my
spectrum display, I want to be able to tell my buddy to transmit in
the middle of that blank spot, not have to calculate a bunch of
offsets in my head.
